The largest European powers at play in Africa included, Belgium, Britain, France, Germany, Italy, Portugal, and Spain. Let’s take a closer look at the territory these powerful entities, their aims in Africa, and the longstanding impact.

Stamp from the Spanish Sahara dated to 1924

Spain claimed a small but powerful position along the continent’s western coast following the Berlin Conference by emerging as a protectorate of two territories known as Rio de Oro and Saguia el-Hamra, later known simply as the Spanish Sahara (envisioned within the modern borders of Western Sahara). Though the Spanish met swift opposition from the local Saharwi culture, Spain was drawn to this region for its incredible abundance of fish. It was not until the twentieth century that Spain began to trek inland and discovered another ample - and incredibly desirable resource: phosphate. It was the desire to maintain access to this abundant commodity that kept Spain invested in the region into the later twentieth century.

Italian Possessions and Sphere of Influence in the Horn of Africa” (1896)

Dominating a massive swath of northern and eastern Africa from the closing years of the nineteenth century until the 1940s, Italy controlled a footprint that today contains the countries of Eritrea, Ethiopia, and Somalia along the eastern Horn of Africa as well as the northern country of Libya. Their draw to the continent began within the shipping industry, when an Italian company was tasked in the 1880s with building a coaling station along the shore of the Red Sea to facilitate passages made possible by the recently constructed Suez Canal. By the decade following, the Italian government had realized the potential of east Africa as a space to expand the Italian empire. So, in addition to gaining control of the territory of Libya, the Italians annexed Eritrea and Somalia. The rise of fascism in the 1930s proved detrimental to relations between Italians and indigenous Africans; the fall of Mussolini and the reinstallation of Ethiopia’s revered emperor, Haile Selassie, in 1941, marked the end of Italy’s African presence.

Representing one of the earliest European imperialist regimes to move into African territory, Belgian King Leopold II began finding a foothold within the region of Congo as early as the 1870s. Hoping to monopolize access to the area’s incredible resources, among them rubber, Leopold II officially annexed the area as the “Congo Free State” the aftermath of the Berlin Conference. Freedom, though, for the people of Congo was far from the king’s mind as his brutal military troupe known as La Force Publique were renowned for their insidious tactics - including mutilation and assault - to keep rubber harvests in line and meeting his imposed quotas, Leopold II maintained a stranglehold on the rubber exports from Africa until 1908, when the power of the region was wrested from his control. The region stayed under the control of Belgium though, until 1960 when it was officially declared an independent country.

Belgium

The British controlled almost the entirety of a swath of Africa that extended from modern Egypt all the way south to modern South Africa; also held territory within West Africa. Their initial domination stemmed from their active role in the slave trade, however by the dawn of the nineteenth century they had abolished the practice which resulted in improved relations. In other parts of British Africa, this peaceful resolution was contrasted with brutal conflict. This was the case for South Africa, where the discovery of diamonds in the 1860s resulted in bloody conflict between the British forces and those of the Dutch Boers. The culmination of this conflict was the Boer War which loomed from 1899 to 1902.

In addition to dominating Algeria, France assumed control of both Tunisia and Morocco in the 1830s, sharing some of the protectorate rights with Spain. Their goal was to tap into the wealth of phosphate, which continues today to be one of Morocco’s richest natural resources and proves essential to the creation of everything from fertilizer to house paint. Germany gobbled up various separate portions of Africa in 1884 but consumed a total terrain of more than 800,000 square kilometers. Their largest territory as German Southwest Africa (modern day Namibia), which while rich in incredible resources from diamonds to fish, was preferred for its expansive (and available) coastline for trade outpost efficiency. Conflicts between the Germans and the indigenous Nama peoples brewed as the Germans confiscated local lands and cattle herds, which resulted in the decimation of the Nama population in the 1890s.

Germany

Portugal was one of Europe’s pioneers in the establishment of trading outposts along the African coast. In the sixteenth century they laid claim to the land that today makes up the southern countries of Angola and Mozambique, but perhaps one of the most striking entanglements between the Portuguese and indigenous African culture along the “Gold Coast” (today comprising the stretch across the countries of the Cote d’Ivoire to Nigeria). Nestled along this coast was the historic Kingdom of Benin, with whom the Portuguese developed a relatively amiable business relationship. In exchange for the export of the wealth of gold and ivory available in the region, the Portuguese also imported vast quantities of brass that the artists of Benin transformed into an exceptionally extensive artistic tradition. The Kingdom of Benin was thus able to keep its independence until the 1897 Benin Expedition launched by the British brought rampant atrocity, the complete destruction of the Oba’s Palace, the decimation of Benin City, and the plundering of much of the kingdom's artistic riches.
