“Value your position, gentlemen. I remind you that King Leopold has the right to exercise enforcement throughout the Congo due to the Arab’s known practice of slavery with the locals. I am certain that you will succeed in the aforementioned task. Go, the vessel awaits you and your men.” Under the starry night sky of the Congo, Khari sighed as gazed into the distance. His eyes narrowed as he hefted his spear. Though he took no pleasure in the killing of these foreigners, he could no longer ignore their crimes. A wounded man tried to crawl to safety, to freedom, before him. Khari closed his eyes and remembered the smell of burning flesh, the screams of innocent victims, and so many other terrible things that he had witnessed in such a short span of time. He tightened his grip on the spear and loosed it. The man before him gasped, once, and fell silent. Khari retrieved his spear and disappeared back into the brush. The attacks, spearheaded by his tribe, were becoming more successful. Though the enemy had impressive weapons, the spear and knowledge of the land would surely always win out. The strong, well-built man led a tribe of native Mongos that rebelled against the Belgian cruelty in the Congo. With this latest victory, he rescued prisoners and slaves that worked on rubber extraction. Many had wounds, missing fingers, or missing hands altogether, the violent price paid to members of the Force Publique; the dead piled higher than he could endure. He could do nothing for them. It seemed there was no limit to Belgian cruelty. Khari spotted a man who remained behind and walked over to him to find the man staring at a tiny child’s hand lying in the dirt. Khari’s stomach clenched. Perhaps this child had been unable to satisfy the desired quota of rubber for a single day; or, perhaps they had done this to punish the parents of the child. He sank beside the man and mourned with him, for no child - or parent - should have had to cope with such injustice. Above all else, Khari loved his people. He was deeply respected and, in these dark times, his tribe looked up to him for guidance and salvation. Though he had no love for violence, the Belgians would not consider a treaty.
