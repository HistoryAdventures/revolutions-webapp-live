Ruling   the   Waves,
Ruling   the   World

Technological developments, powered by the industrial and scientific revolutions of the 19th century, encouraged ever broader colonial horizons for European nations. Better ships, more effective navigation tools, and the desire for expansion and economic exploitation led the European nations to look further afield to the continents of Asia and Australia.

Chartered
companies

‘East India House’, (ca.1800) Thomas Malton the Younger. A painting of the expanded façade ofhte East India House in London gives a sense of the incredible wealth of the Company, as well as its sense of importance:

  • One of the key agents in the European colonisation and economic exploitation of the Asian continent were the chartered companies, including famous examples such as the British East India Company (founded 1600 and informally known just as ‘The Company’) and the Dutch East India Company (founded 1602, recognised by the abbreviation ‘VOC’ meaning Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie).
  • These commercial organisations enjoyed special privileges that were granted by the state. This was typically in the form of a royal charter. Their growth in number, size, and significance is directly related to the European exploration of the New Worlds to the west and east, and the economic exploitation of the resources they discovered there. Such was their prominence in certain regions, and the importance of their economic hold of an area, that these companies becomes political and imperial organisations, as well as commercial enterprises. This happened for example with the British East India Company.
  • The prominence of these companies within their respective nations meant that the economic policy and goals of the two were frequently entwined. Frequently, from the 16th through to 19th centuries, the prevailing beliefs can be categorised under the term mercantilism. This mindset postulated that the earth’s resources and wealth were finite, and that a state’s power depended entirely upon its ability to control as much of both as possible. It is not hard to understand how this fed into the ideology of imperial expansion that fuelled the European nations at this time.

Empire

The Convention of Chuenpee (ca.1841). This is the first page of the first attempted agreement between the British Empire and the Qing Dynasty made during the First Opium War. Although unratified, many of the terms in the Convention of Chuenpee were included in the Treaty of Nanking. These included the cession of Hong Kong island and harbor to the British Crown and the opening of the port of Canton to trade.

  • The pursual of gunboat diplomacy could have consequences far beyond the enforced economic exploitation of a nation. There were instances of it contributing to the direct expansion of an Empire. The island of Hong Kong for instance was occupied by the British in 1841 during the First Opium War. The British victory, and their ability to dictate the terms of surrender, meant that the island was formerly ceded to the British Empire by the Qing dynasty in 1842. Further British consolidation of their new colonial acquisition occurred throughout the 19th century, including its classification as a Crown Colony in 1843, and the expansion into the Kowloon Peninsula in 1860, after the Second Opium War.
  • With a base of operations in the Far East, the British could intensify their imperial endeavors in the region. This included the Treaty of Tientsin in 1860, leading to the Chinese being compelled to open more ports to the British, licenses for the British to navigate the Yangtze River, and a reaffirmation of the legalization of the Opium Trade. Hong Kong would not be returned to Chinese governance until 1997. The “prizes” the Europeans won through gunboat diplomacy were not willingly let go, indicating how significant they were in terms of economic benefits, and the prestige they were perceived to offer.

Competition

‘Save me from my friends!’, (1878), Punch Magazine. The Afghan Emir Sher Al Khan is squashed between his ‘friends’, Russia (the Bear) and the British Empire (Lion). Expansion for strategic and economic gain led to competition and tensions between the great European states:

  • The colonial expansion of European states into the Asian continent for largely economic reasons was not limited to merely one nation, nor was it a phenomenon of the 18th and 19th centuries only. Portuguese and Spanish traders had reached the islands of South East Asia as early as the 16th century, where their victories over Arab, Chinese and other Asian forces allowed them to establish a lucrative maritime empire based upon the movement of goods, notably spices.
  • With envious eyes cast at the empires of the Iberian nations, other European states rapidly mobilised. In much the same way as there was to be a ‘Scramble for Africa’ in the 19th century, the lucrative lands of Asia were greedily gobbled up by European colonisers or forcibly opened up to trade through Gunboat Diplomacy. Notable examples of this came with the Dutch control of Indonesia, the British establishing rule in India, and the French conquest of Indochina (the area roughly equivalent to modern Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos). The latter two instance are examples of what can be classified as “New Imperialism”, which refers to a change in approach from indirect control, to the formal establishment of colonial rule over an overseas territory.
  • Conquest and exploitation were predominately motivated by the drive for commercial growth and nationalism, and the effects of this would be felt well into the 20th century and beyond as colonised states sought independence and to plot their own course of national destiny.
