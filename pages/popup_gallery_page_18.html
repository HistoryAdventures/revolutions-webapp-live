Friends of the People, Isaac Cruikshank November 15, 1792

Paine’s philosophy and power of connecting his ideas to ordinary people saw him have a profound influence on not only the American, but also the French Revolution. This caricature from 1792 shows how Paine (right) was perceived to be a danger to society. Incendiary ideas stack up around munitions, weapons and the devil himself as Paine sows his ideas. Representation in political cartoons such as these testify to how the common people knew who Paine was, and that certain sectors of society wished to vilify him and his ideas.

Title page from first edition of Two Treatises of Government

Note that the Locke’s ideas concerning the nature of government and politics was originally published anonymously in this book in 1680. Locke actually considered the ideas that he had committed to the page, which challenged the established political status quo, to be so dangerous that he never acknowledged that he was the author in his lifetime. Imagine writing one of the most influential books in history and never claiming credit for it!

Soldiers of the 1st Rhode Island Regiment of the Continental Army, Jean-Baptiste-Antoine DeVerger, (1781)

The 1st Rhode Island Regiment, here shown on the Yorktown Campaign (1781), had the largest number of black Patriot soldiers in the continental army. Here they are shown fighting in uniform alongside white comrades, willing to die for the same ideals and principles of equality and freedom. Nevertheless, although some states (particularly in the north) abolished slavery soon after the war, racial inequality and violence remained a festering problem, and one that would not be healed peacefully.

The able Doctor, or America Swallowing the Bitter Draught, The London Magazine (1st May 1774)

This satirical political cartoon shows an allegorical figure of America, the central female, being held down by various members of British Parliament. Collectively they are forcing tea down her throat, a reference to the Boston Port Bill, which is being held by one of the Parliamentarians. This was the bill passed by the British that fined the Americans for the destruction of the tea during the Boston Tea Party. In the background, Britannia herself leans on her spear and looks away in shame, as her representatives continue to exploit America (note that one of the parliamentarians is also looking up her skirt).

Page from the codebook of the Culper Spy Ring, late 1770’s

General George Washington was desperate for an organized, effective intelligence service to help the patriot war efforts. This was especially true after several of his earlier spies had been captured and executed by the British forces. Here, a page of the Culper Ring’s code book makes clear that Washington eventually achieved his ambition. The left-hand column displays a series of people and places, whilst in the adjacent column there are the numerical and alphabetical codes which allowed the members of the ring to communicate important information. If you look closely, you can see that New York is represented by the numeric code 727.
