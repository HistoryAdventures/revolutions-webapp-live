Treaty of Paris

  • Signed 3rd September, 1783, by representatives of King George III of Great Britain and the Representatives of United States of America.
  • Article 1 of the treaty acknowledges the United States’ existence as free and sovereign. This is the only article of the treaty still in force!
  • Set the boundaries between the British Empire in North America and the new United States.
  • The treaty also granted fishing rights to the US, the return of prisoners of war, and the lawful payment of debts on both sides.
  • Britain also signed treaties with France, Spain, and the Dutch – all of whom had supported the American cause – and these came to be known, collectively, as the Peace of Paris.

Constitution

  • Created 17th September 1787
  • Usually regarded as the oldest written and codified national constitution still in force. Has been amended 27 times since it came into force in 1789 – it has been developed to meet the changing needs of the nation.
  • Outlines the separation of powers in federal government: 3 branches of governmental power: the legislative (the US Congress), the executive (the President) and the judicial (the Supreme Court).
  • Displays the influences of the ideas of Montesquieu and Locke in particular.”

We the people?

  • Although the first words of the Constitution proclaim that the new United States’ government was aimed at protecting all its citizens, there are debates over to what extent this was realized.
  • American women benefitted to a degree from the ideals of the Revolution. The concept of Republican Motherhood emerged, and women were seen as essential figures in the communication of the values and virtues of good citizens to the next generation. Although New Jersey briefly gave political rights to women, there was to be no universal suffrage for women.
  • The newly founded states were also inspired by the ideals of the Revolutionary period to free numerous slaves, but this tended to be along a north/south divide in terms of extent.
  • Whilst the northern states had constitutions that either advocated equal rights or abolished slavery (New York had abolished slavery by 1827), in the southern states it frequently came down to personal choice.

United States Declaration of Independence (July 4th, 1776)

  • Approved by Continental Congress on July 4th 1776 in Pennsylvania State House (Independence Hall, Philadelphia).
  • Announced that the Thirteen Colonies were at War with Great Britain (12 voted in favor, New York abstained).
  • Written by Thomas Jefferson and directly influenced by Paine’s Common Sense.
  • Allowed the recognition of the United States by friendly foreign governments.
  • Document stated that all men are created equal and had certain inalienable rights, including the right to life, to liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

Montesquieu (1689-1755)

  • Charles Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu – French scholar and essayist.
  • The Spirit of the Laws (1748).
  • The theory of the separation of powers.
  • The best way to secure liberty and prevent corruption was to divide the powers among different branches (i.e. between the legislative and the executive).

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778)

  • Influenced Thomas Paine.
  • The Social Contract (1762).
  • Insisted that citizens have a right of self-government, choosing the rules of their society and the judges who will enforce those rules.
  • Advocated a form of government with less institutions – for the relationship between the will of the state and the will of the people (the so-called General Will) to be democratic, it needs to be overseen or mediated by as few as institutions as possible..
