‘’In the Rubber Coils. Scene – the Congo ‘Free’ State,” cartoon by Edward Linley Sambourne published in Punch magazine on 28 November 1906.

In this satirical cartoon – the sort typically found in news weeklies like Punch – a man is seemingly strangled by a coil of rubber that culminates in an unflattering profile portrait of a king. A biting critique of European imperialism and its effect in Africa, these types of cartoons reminded those in Europe of the mistreatments that were occurring.

Map of the Congo River (River Zaire), as illustrated in James H. Tuckey’s narrative of an Expedition to Explore the River Zaire in 1816 (published 1818)

While figures like Livingstone made their names on exploring Africa, they were not the first to do so. One early pioneer of the African landscape was James Hingston Tuckey (1776-1816), an Irish explorer who set out in February 1816 to explore the Congo River. His efforts unfortunately proved inconsequential – he could not navigate the river in its entirety – and he died later that same year in a town along Congo’s coast.

David Livingstone (1864)

David Livingstone (1813-1873) was from Scotland and was a trained doctor, however it was his feats as an explorer that earned him eternal fame. Originally focused on tracing the origins of the Nile River, Livingstone also became fixated on documenting uncharted portions of central Africa. It was on this latter mission that Livingstone disappeared for more than half a decade, only to resurface in 1871.
