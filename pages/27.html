Wants

Father Thames Introducing His Offspring to the Fair City of London, (1858), Punch Magazine. People were desperate to escape poverty in overcrowded, disease-ridden cities in industrial Europe

Wants

  • The demographic boom of the nineteenth century in Europe meant that competition for resources – be that food, money, or simply space (land to live on and off). The Industrial Revolution had caused an increase in crime, motivated largely by the resultant economic disparity as societies struggled to work out how to come to terms in an age of industry. The vast spaces of the new lands discovered by Captain Cook and others offered scope for European expansion and development to alleviate these burdens.

Competition

The ‘Betsy Ross’ flag, one of the very first flags of the United States (1777). The advent of American independence and loss of a colony motivated a new drive for British colonial expansion.

Competition

  • With the loss of the American colonies, the balance of European power could have been severely disrupted. It was imperative for British interests that the territory – and the resources, manpower, and security if offered – was adequately replaced. These broader imperialist concerns motivated European exploration, as land was sought as part of the great imperial game.

Toleration

Fenians Escape by Whaleboat to the Catalpa (1876), unknown artist. A group of 6 Fenians (Irish Nationalists) attempt to flee the penal colony in western Australia

Toleration

  • In much the same way that many of the first settlers in the North American colonies had sought refuge from the prevailing religious beliefs, so too was toleration a key motivation in later European migration. Now, however, it was differing political opinions rather than religious beliefs. Historical estimates have suggested for example that as many as 1 in 7 of the penal convicts were political prisoners.

Crime

Crime

  • Famously, many of the first arrivals in Australia were convicts. This was a process that had been ongoing since the 17th century, but American Independence prompted the search for a new solution. Captain Cook’s discovery of Australia offered just such an answer. In reality, the majority of the convicts transported were held for petty crimes (serious crimes were punishable by death for instance).
  • Upon serving their time, the emancipated prisoners often remained in Australia to build new lives for themselves.

Wealth

Prospectors by ST Gill. 3 men can be seen panning for gold in an Australian river during the Gold Rush

Wealth

  • The vast new lands opened up by the European forces offered scope for economic exploitation in myriad ways. The land itself, when space was increasingly becoming a premium in Europe, could be exploited for agriculture or urban development. Similarly, there was an increasing awareness of other ways the wealth of the earth could be extracted. In Australia, and later as part of the westward expansion in America, colonizers were seduced by the “Gold Rush” phenomenon; in this case, the promise of riches quite literally drove expansion.

Opportunity

Landing of Captain Cook at Botany Bay, 1770, (1902), E. Phillips Fox. Produced more than a century after the First Fleet landed, this image indicates how the colonization was a cause for celebration in the Empire

Opportunity

  • The main draw for European expansion this period was the promise of opportunity. This took on many different forms. Primarily, the discovery and colonization of new lands allowed European powers to ‘steal a march’ on their imperial competitors. The acquisition of a rich and populated new land could help a country grow rich in comparison to its rivals.

Evangelizing

Richard Johnson, chaplain of the First Fleet (1787). Johnson can be seen at work in the lower panel. A strong religious element was present from the first days of colonization

Evangelizing

  • Encountering the natives of the new lands meant that the European colonizers were brought into contact with native peoples with rich traditions and cultures of their own. Although at first there were attempts at interaction and cooperation, this was soon surpassed by exploitation and re-education. Indigenous people were compelled to renounce their traditional beliefs and way of life and accept instead the imported faiths and creeds of the colonizers. Where the colonizers went, the churches were never far behind.
