This image gives an excellent - albeit satirical - impression of the opinion the first colonizers of Australia were held in. In reality, a proportion of the criminals sent to the other side of the world were actually held on largely trivial charges.

Australian gold diggings, by Edwin Stocqueler, (c. 1855)

This painting offers a picturesque idyll of colonial life in nineteenth century Australia. The “Gold Rush” is in full effect, as the European settlers can be seen to attempt to grow rich of the exploitation of the natural resources of the colony. Look closely, and you’ll notice there is not an aboriginal in sight, while the contrast between verdant green and desecrated brown suggests the negative impact all these men are having on the world around them.

The Kongouro from New Holland, George Stubbs (1772)

This oil painting by George Stubbs is the first verified depiction of an Australian animal in western art. It was based on the inflated skin of an animal (unfortunately not a living Kangaroo) collected by Joseph Banks, who had been a part of Captain Cook’s first voyage of discovery, encountering the east coast of Australia in 1770. The Kongouro as depicted by Stubbs looks only passably similar to how we know Kangaroos to look, but the painting captures the sense of mystery and excitement felt at the prospect of a new world to be explored.

Beer Street and Gin Lane, William Hogarth (1751)

Part of a pair of prints, here the 18th century English artist William Hogarth turns his scalpel-sharp satirical eye for detail on the depravation that was everywhere visible on the streets of the cities of Britain during the industrial revolution. Produced to support the Gin Act (1750), the artist’s work is not merely a critique of the consumption of the spirit. The streets of the city are crowded, dirty, and full of crime; the more you look, the more you discover. Holding the viewer’s attention is the central figure of the mother however. The sores on her legs suggest syphilis and prostitution, whilst she seems unperturbed by her falling child, concerned only with having another pinch of snuff (tobacco). Although satirical, Hogarth’s work conveys most clearly the push factors that drove people Britain to the new world of Australia. The new world in the southern ocean offered an escape.

A General Chart of New Holland including New South Wales & Botany Bay with the Adjacent Countries and New Discovered Lands (1786)

Published one year prior to the arrival of the First Fleet in Botany Bay, this map of the Australasian region gives an impression of the extensive European surveying that had been underway for years previous to the plans to colonize. Although the map lacks specificity, the shape of the landmasses are easily identifiable. Although Australia represented a New World for those arriving on the First Fleet and the years after, this map makes clear the extent of European exploration in the region.
