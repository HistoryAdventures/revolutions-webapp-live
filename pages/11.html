    I am not a soldier. I will never wield a musket nor face cannon fire. My place is not upon the battlements or racing through the battlefield.
    I am a spy, intent on serving my young nation as it bursts from its British womb. I take personal pride in saving it from itself, and yet there are only so many things I can do. I am a woman, a slave, and unworthy in the eyes of so many, but who better to sneak and creep throughout the halls, listening to conversations as I fill bathtubs and stoke up hearths for the chilly evenings?
    We have a traitor in our midst. Captain Arnold has been feeding the British information from the front, the recruits, and even from here at home. Arnold is a turncoat and nothing more, but he is also a friend of my master. John Andre is a good man, a rather fine man if you’re asking me despite his support of the British.
    If the rebels knew that my master was speaking with Captain Arnold, they might make an attempt on his life. It would expose me and force other possible turncoats from his home. There is so much chatter at evening events or simple get-togethers. I have heard so much at my position that I have reported to Townsend, to Saratoga, with the hopes of it reaching Washington himself.
    This new nation is to be free and though I am a slave, I know that one day that will mean freedom for us, too. For a nation of slavery cannot stand and call itself free. So long as we have collars around our necks and chains around our ankles, our free country has not yet been realized.
    I wrinkle my nose as I walk into the kitchen. I fetch a serving tray and head back into the drawing room, filled with men and women dressed in finery. My sharp eyes catch a glimpse of the man I’ve been reporting upon and my breath tightens. He sees me watching him and I hurry to look away. I hope, in his arrogance, he’ll just think me stunned by his presence. He’s fought in many battles, and he’s surely accustomed to the ladies, both free and slave, gasping over him.
    I glance at him out of the corner of my eye and see his smug, self-assured smirk. I hope it’s the last time I have to endure it.
